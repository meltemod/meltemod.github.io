Prescription drug abuse is an ongoing public health crisis in the US, and ?doctor shopping? is one of the primary means of obtaining prescription opioids. However, prescription drug seeking (PDS) behavior is difficult to identify, and there is little agreement about how to operationalize it. Moreover, drug seeking strategies have likely evolved over the course of the 15-year opioid epidemic in conjunction with policy changes, necessitating new measures for capturing fraudulent behavior. Yet, no existing study has systematically compared the predictive utility of PDS indicators longitudinally. Our research fills this gap, and also introduces a social network indicator of PDS. We compare the performance of PageRank - a measure of the prominence of patients in co-prescription networks - to that of traditional indicators of PDS in predicting opioid abuse and overdose over a 10-year period. Our analysis uses deidentified health claims data from a large commercially insured population between 2009-2018.
